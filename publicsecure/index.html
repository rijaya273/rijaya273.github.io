Public Secure

ODS Lab, exploring single sign-on design

Public secure is a government team that leads the initiative to create single sign-on for online applications across the government of Ontario. As the consulting team, we introduced user-centric design by demonstrating the importance of research and synthesis, while developing discovery phase insights and prototype screens.

Understanding

establishing context

Stakeholder insight
13 stakeholders were interviewed, whom were all members of different program areas that have the potential to implement a better single sign-on system.

Key takeaways from analyzing the existing user flows:

  • login frustrations stem from confusing onboarding processes and a lack of hierarchy in the presentation of information.
  • relatively small application access frequency further accentuates frustration upon login.
  • although the job to be done is effectively application access, different program areas nuances required consideration.

Stakeholder interviews created a basis for understanding the problem space and further insight, which was then taken into consideration for generative research.

End user insight

Furthermore, 12 potential end users from the general public were interviewed. Analyzing their various characteristics and views resulted in the below spectrum, considering the following attributes that were decided on via stakeholder knowledge gaps:

  • system expectations
  • government participation
  • technology reception and comprehension
  • account attitudes

Synthesizing

generating guidance

As a result of what we learned via stakeholder and user interviews, it was determined that all ranges of the spectrums must be considered. Thus, for the discovery phase of Public Secure, designing for generality was the guiding principle for success.

To further display designing for generality as a guide for success, vague personas were drawn out for purpose of tangible illustration. Rather than pinpoint a specific user group the problem is being solved for, this particular persona exercise emphasized the consideration of the many edge cases. This frame of mind was then applied by considering a variety of user flows and different user groups, translating them to subsequent feature sets.

Prototyping and Evaluating

materializing and testing ideas

After conducting generative research, we mapped out our general understanding of what flows needed to be demonstrated in the discovery phase. The initial interpretation were these 4 core scenarios:

  • enrolment as a net new user
  • enrolment as returning single sign-on user
  • logging in to application area
  • password reset

Some key changes made throughout several iterations:

Presentation of sign in options & other information:

It was important to strike a balance between presenting enough information and not overwhelming the general majority. This was based on the fact that there are users whom are very familiar with government processes and sign-in technology, and those whom are less so.

  • Minimizing callout verbiage created for more seamless skimming.
  • Opting for radio buttons provided a more comprehensive and direct instruction for Ontarians.
  • Striking the right balance was essential to design for generality.

The order of application enrolment:

A series of twelve usability tests helped determine that expected screens should come as soon as possible, rather than later. This emulates expected patterns in order to establish more trust in government systems and set the stage for more technology receptiveness. In addition, this decision sought to contribute to a sense of control for those on the more wary side of the security spectrum.

  • Reordering account creation and enrolment pins for the latter to come first was an essential change
  • It was important to consider how general orientation within flows will cater to everyone.

Reimagining onboard architecture:

The initial setup of having sign-in options presented upfront was problematic in that it was being interpreted as different account types.

  • Changing account order allowed the presentation of sign-in options as a setting within an account, rather than the essence of the account in itself.
  • This was the desired product model upon learning of the user need for control over account variables, a consideration needed for all ends of the spectrums, as mentioned prior.

Delivery

communicating concepts

Although this was a discovery phase project, the scenarios we explored were effective nonetheless in establishing principles for further takes on single sign-on design. Qualitative success was found by communicating generalist principles for single sign-on design, which we found would accommodate for the vast user group that is the general public.

Towards the end of the iterations and usability tests, it was demonstrated that by catering towards individual comments and opinions, the final product veered towards one that was more digestible for users on all parts of the spectrums.

Personal Learnings

takeaways

  • Establishing higher level questions & analogies for mental models is super valuable to asking users the right generative questions.

  • Discovery phase projects give opportunity to dabble in lots of design processes in-depth, generative and evaluative.

  • Content design is a LOT more than just the effective words. Projects such as Public secure are dysfunctional without well thought out content and navigation. Although this might imply frustrating debates over subtle nuances, it is a well worthwhile exercise to apply throughout public service!
